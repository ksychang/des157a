Form Design

October 14, 2025

Form design is a critical yet often overlooked area of UI/UX that can play a major role in the conversion rate of a website or application. Filling out a form can be a dreary and sometimes impossible task, but with good design choices we can make it a more streamlined and even enjoyable experience.

This article outlines some great best practices for form design to implement. One tip I found really interesting was to not make labels inline, but above or to the left of the field. This is because when the user enters input, the label disappears and the user might forget what they were entering into the field. I have never put much thought into the positioning of field labels, so reading about where they should be placed made me much more aware of the subtlety of these practices. My takeaway from the 20 tips given in the article was that our main goal should be to minimize any potential annoyances or pain points the user might encounter so their focus can remain on their original task.

One website I recently used that I think implements these form design practices well is Urban Outfitters. They follow all the best practices for labeling, layout, error messages, indicating required fields, and displaying the multiple steps needed via a progress bar.

Overlays Design Pattern Research

October 23, 2025

Modals are a UI pattern that were created to address the issue of limited screen space. When we want the user’s attention or input, or to show them additional information that may or may not be related to the main content, modal windows can sometimes be used as an effective solution.

However, given some of the problems surrounding modals, there are several considerations that should be made before using them. They can interrupt user flow, especially when they are not triggered by a user action. Poor implementation of modals have also trained users to automatically close them, which can defeat their original purpose.

Some best practices with using modals, if they are deemed the most appropriate solution, are to ensure they have good anatomy and accessibility. A well designed modal will have an intuitive way to close it, descriptive title, clear purpose or corresponding action, and proper placement in the window.

Reading this article reminded me how important it is to take UX into consideration when designing UI. As someone who loves to focus on the visuals and perfecting little details, it is humbling to know that if a user has been trained to ignore the component I am designing, my work will be useless. In my career, I want to make sure that I am open to solutions beyond what is traditionally used in order to create the most effective designs possible.

Visual Thinking Strategies Research

October 28, 2025

I’ve always viewed visual thinking as a kind of competition with myself, where I challenge my synthesizing skills and ask, “How can I see beyond what’s here at face value?” Unfortunately, I’m not sure that I’m the best at reading between the lines and interpreting the details I see in images. However, I do think this is a skill I can develop over time, and I have seen some improvements over time as I keep challenging myself to think more critically about images.

In terms of visual thinking, I find this website to have a really interesting interactive element with images. In the second section of the page, a trail of images appears as you move the cursor around. Each image is different in size, shape, and content. I think it encourages visual thinking because it makes the viewer consider how each of those images relates to the story of the design collective. The collection of images as a whole help provide a sense of who the brand is and what they’re all about. I think this element could be a fun addition to my project if I can figure out how to implement it.

Visual Thinking Analysis

October 30, 2025

Saul’s images are about pathways, places where people travel or move through. This particular image is an outdoor path. For me, I find it interesting that the path is completely empty at what looks like midday. I also noticed there is a stop sign at the end of the path, and a wire fence running along the sides.

My suggestions to Saul were that if he were to rephotograph this image, he could consider taking it at different heights to indicate different perspectives, such as someone taller or perhaps a dog being walked on the path. I said that a potential concept to explore is the idea that even though we all walk along the same path, our experiences of the journey can be vastly different.






This is one of my favorite images in my collection. I took it in the house I am currently living in, when we were first moving in and the living room was still clean. The late afternoon sunlight beams in through the blinds onto the floor. One of my housemates is by the window, unpacking a box of her things sitting on the couch.

To me, this moment I captured represents transitions in time, space, and life. Moving into a new house was an exciting but also scary transition, and not knowing how relationships and routines would pan out was uncomfortable. In the same way the afternoon sun was transitioning to the evening, the sun was beginning to set on my previous chapter in college, and I was moving onto a new one.

To make this image more compelling, I might consider cropping it to focus more on my housemate and the sunbeams on the floor. This would reduce the other details in the room, like the couch, door, ceiling fan, shelves, and hanging jackets. For some of my other images I think this would be beneficial, but for others they are better off with all the details contained in the photo.
